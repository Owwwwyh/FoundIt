1. Problem Statement & Target Users

On a busy university campus, hundreds of items are lost every semester — student ID and access cards, keys, water bottles, calculators, chargers, lab coats, and electronics. Today these are reported through scattered, informal channels: WhatsApp and Telegram class groups, word of mouth, and paper notices pinned to faculty boards. These channels share three weaknesses: they are not searchable, they are ephemeral (a message scrolls away within hours), and they are uncoordinated (the person who lost an item and the person who found it are rarely in the same group). As a result, found items seldom reach their owners, and the security/administration office has no single record to consult.

FoundIt solves this by giving the campus one searchable, persistent, and secure noticeboard for lost and found items, with a verifiable claim process so items are returned to the right person.

Target users

  • Owners who lost an item — search the found-items list and post a "lost" report describing what is missing.
  • Finders (students, staff, security) — post a "found" item so its owner can locate it.
  • Claimants & posters — a person files a claim with proof of ownership; the original poster reviews and approves or rejects it.
Why it is feasible & meaningful: the audience (campus community) is the same audience that will watch the demo, the problem is real and relatable, and the data model is small enough to build completely within the semester while still exercising every required topic — SPA, REST CRUD, related tables, and JWT-protected access.

The application is built from four coherent modules. Together they deliver complete CRUD across the data model and at least one JWT-protected flow.

Out of scope (intentional, to stay realistic)

To keep the scope deliverable in 2–3 weeks, the following are deliberately excluded and noted as future work: real-time chat between users, AI image recognition of items, email/push notifications, and a native mobile app. Optional stretch goal if time allows: a single photo upload per item.

3.3 Database schema (3 related tables)

Every record hangs off users. A user posts many items; each item receives many claims; a user files many claims. All relationships are one-to-many, enforced by foreign keys.

3.5 Security & validation

  • JWT authentication: login issues a signed token; a Slim middleware verifies the Authorization: Bearer <token> header on every protected route and returns 401 if missing or invalid.
  • Authorization (ownership): editing/deleting an item or ruling on a claim checks that the token's user owns the resource — otherwise 403 Forbidden (distinct from 401).
  • Password storage: passwords are hashed with password_hash() (bcrypt); plaintext is never stored.
  • Input validation on both ends: Vue validates forms before submitting (required fields, email format, length); PHP re-validates every request before touching the database and returns 422 with field messages on failure.
  • SQL-injection defence: all queries use PDO prepared statements with bound parameters.
  • Transport & CORS: served over HTTPS; the API sets explicit CORS headers for the deployed frontend origin.
